From Drone to Self-Authored: A Personal Note on Becoming

Mar 1, 2026 · 6 min read

I’m done living like a drone.

For too long, I’ve lived in ways that made me useful to everyone else while quietly costing me myself. Productive on paper. Empty in private.

This post is my line in the sand: I want a life that is not outsourced to other people’s expectations. I want to become the author of it.

What I mean by “drone mode”

Drone mode is not laziness. It’s compliance. It’s waking up and reacting to demands, requests, notifications, and social scripts until the day is over. You can look busy, even successful, while drifting further from your own center.

In drone mode, your time is full but your life is under-lived. You become efficient at maintaining what already exists, not creating what should exist.

The philosophical anchor: Nietzsche’s “Übermensch”

The word Übermensch is often misunderstood. I’m not using it as superiority over others. I’m using it as a challenge to myself: stop inheriting your values passively and start creating them consciously.

In practical terms, this means refusing to live by default settings: inherited fears, borrowed goals, and performative success. It means becoming—not pretending.

My reinterpretation (for real life)

For me, “becoming” means three things:

1) Self-respect over social approval.
Not every request deserves my life energy. Boundaries are not selfish; they are structural.

2) Creation over maintenance.
Every day should include at least one act of creation that moves my own life forward.

3) Responsibility over excuses.
If I keep repeating a pattern, I own it. If I own it, I can change it.

The cost of not changing

If I stay in drone mode, nothing dramatic happens overnight. That’s the danger. The cost is paid slowly: less courage, less originality, less aliveness.

You wake up one day with a life that looks acceptable but doesn’t feel true. I don’t want that ending.

My operating rules from now on

Rule 1: Start each day with one self-authored objective before I touch noise.

Rule 2: If a task serves everyone but me, I question it.

Rule 3: I ship one meaningful thing daily—however small.

Rule 4: I track proof, not mood.

Rule 5: I close each day by asking: “Did I live deliberately today?”

This is not rebellion for rebellion’s sake

I still want to contribute. I still want to be useful. But I refuse to build a life where my usefulness to others is purchased by abandoning myself.

The point is integration: build a life that helps others because it is aligned—not while self-erasing.

Final note to myself

Becoming is not one dramatic moment. It is repetition. Decisions repeated become identity. Identity repeated becomes destiny.

So this is the commitment: less autopilot, less compliance, less drift.

More authorship. More courage. More life.
